How to Start an Internet Radio Station in 2026

By TaterLabs · April 2026 · 8 min read

Starting an internet radio station used to require expensive equipment, dedicated servers, and a team of engineers. In 2026, you can do it from a single Windows PC in about 5 minutes. This guide walks you through everything — from choosing your format to going live.

What You Need to Get Started

Here's the minimum to launch a real, listenable internet radio station:

  • A Windows PC — Windows 10 or 11. Doesn't need to be powerful.
  • Music files — MP3 or FLAC. Your own collection, royalty-free music, or licensed tracks.
  • Radio automation software — this is what runs your station 24/7, schedules music, and handles streaming.
  • An internet connection — even a basic home connection works for small audiences.

That's it. No rack-mounted servers. No audio mixers. No broadcast license for internet-only stations in the US.

Step 1: Choose Your Station Format

Before you touch any software, decide what your station is about. Format is everything in radio — it's what makes listeners come back.

  • Music-focused — indie rock, jazz, electronic, lo-fi, country. Pick a niche.
  • Talk/podcast hybrid — scheduled shows mixed with music blocks.
  • Community station — local news, events, and music from your area.
  • College radio — eclectic mix, student DJs, campus events.
  • Church/ministry — sermons, worship music, community programming.

The tighter your format, the more loyal your audience. "We play everything" means nobody has a reason to tune in.

Step 2: Get Your Music Library Ready

You need content to fill airtime. For a 24/7 station, aim for at least 200-300 tracks to start — that gives you roughly 15-20 hours before repeating.

Where to Get Music

  • Your own collection — rip your CDs or use MP3s you've purchased.
  • Royalty-free libraries — sites like Free Music Archive, Incompetech, and Artlist offer music you can broadcast legally.
  • Independent artists — many indie musicians will happily let you play their music for exposure. Reach out directly.
  • Licensing services — BMI, ASCAP, and SoundExchange handle licensing for commercial use. Costs vary.

Important: If you're streaming copyrighted music publicly, you need proper licensing. For non-commercial, educational, or personal stations, the rules are more relaxed — but check your local regulations.

Step 3: Choose Your Radio Software

This is the biggest decision you'll make. Your automation software is the brain of your station — it schedules music, manages playlists, handles streaming, and keeps your station running 24/7.

The Old Way (Expensive)

Traditional radio automation software like RadioTrafficE, WideOrbit, or RCS Zetta costs $3,000 to $15,000+ with annual maintenance fees. These are built for commercial FM stations with full engineering teams.

The Free Way (Limited)

Open source options like Liquidsoap, AzuraCast, or Mixxx exist but require technical knowledge — you'll be editing config files, managing servers, and troubleshooting command-line tools. Great if you're a developer. Not great if you just want to run a station.

Step 4: Set Up Your Stream

Your radio software needs somewhere to send the audio so listeners can tune in. This is called a "streaming server" or "mount point."

Free Streaming Options

  • Built-in streaming — some software (like TaterWave AI) includes streaming capability built right in. No external server needed.
  • Icecast — free, open-source streaming server you can run on your own machine or a VPS.
  • Shoutcast — another popular option with a free tier for small stations.

Paid Streaming Services

  • Live365 — starts around $59/month. Handles licensing and streaming.
  • RadioKing — starts around $29/month. Web-based management.
  • Airtime Pro — cloud-based automation and streaming combo.

Step 5: Go Live

Once your software is configured and your stream is set up:

  1. Load your music library into the automation software.
  2. Set up your schedule — what plays when.
  3. Start the stream.
  4. Share your station URL with the world.

That's it. You're a radio station.

Step 6: Grow Your Audience

Having a station is step one. Getting listeners is the ongoing work:

  • Social media — post what's playing, share your station link.
  • Station directories — list on TuneIn, Radio.net, and internet radio directories.
  • Listener app — give people a branded app they can install on their phone.
  • Consistent schedule — listeners come back when they know what to expect.
  • Community — live chat, request lines, and interaction keep people engaged.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a license to run an internet radio station?

In the US, internet-only stations don't need an FCC broadcast license. However, if you play copyrighted music publicly, you may need licenses from performance rights organizations (BMI, ASCAP, SESAC) and SoundExchange for digital performance royalties.

How much does it cost to run an internet radio station?

You can start for free with open-source tools, or as low as $29/month with modern automation software. The main costs are software, streaming bandwidth, and music licensing if applicable.

Can I run a radio station from my home computer?

Yes. Modern radio automation software is designed to run on regular Windows PCs. You don't need a dedicated server or special hardware.

How many listeners can I have?

It depends on your streaming setup. Built-in streaming on a home connection can handle 10-50+ concurrent listeners. External streaming services can handle thousands.
